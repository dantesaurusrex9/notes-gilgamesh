# 6 - Counterfactuals and Sanity Checks

[toc]

> **TL;DR:** Counterfactual explanations answer "what is the minimal change to this input that would flip the model's decision?" — a qualitatively different and more actionable question than attribution maps. FIDO (Chang et al., ICLR 2019) formalizes this via generative infilling with SDR (smallest deletion region) and SSR (smallest supporting region). Crucially, Adebayo et al. (NeurIPS 2018) demonstrate that many widely-used saliency methods — including Guided BackProp and Guided Grad-CAM — fail elementary sanity checks: their outputs are statistically independent of both model parameters and data labels, rendering them useless for faithful explanation.

## Vocabulary

**Counterfactual explanation**: A minimal modification to the input $x$ that changes the model's prediction from the observed class $c$ to a different class $c'$. Answers "what would need to be different for this decision to go the other way?"

---

**Smallest Deletion Region (SDR)**: FIDO's deletion objective — the smallest image region that, when removed (replaced by an infilled alternative), causes the model to change its prediction or drop below a confidence threshold $\tau$.

```math
\text{SDR} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{m}} \|\mathbf{m}\|_1 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad f_c(I \odot (1-\mathbf{m}) + G \odot \mathbf{m}) < \tau
```

---

**Smallest Supporting Region (SSR)**: FIDO's complementary objective — the smallest image region that, when presented alone (everything else infilled), is sufficient to achieve model confidence $\geq \tau$ for the predicted class.

```math
\text{SSR} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{m}} \|\mathbf{m}\|_1 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad f_c(I \odot \mathbf{m} + G \odot (1-\mathbf{m})) \geq \tau
```

---

**Generative infilling**: In FIDO, the replaced region $G = \text{CA-GAN}(I, \mathbf{m})$ is generated by a context-aware GAN conditioned on the unmasked pixels, producing a semantically coherent infill that remains on the data manifold (unlike grey-patch or blurred replacement).

---

**Bernoulli dropout mask**: FIDO's optimization variable — a spatial binary mask $\mathbf{m} \in \{0,1\}^{H \times W}$ relaxed to $[0,1]^{H \times W}$ via the reparameterization trick and rounded at inference. The mask is differentiable with respect to the infill objective, enabling gradient-based optimization.

---

**Model parameter randomization test (Adebayo et al.)**: A sanity check that cascades random re-initialization through the layers of the model, from the last layer back to the first. A faithful saliency method should produce increasingly different maps as more layers are randomized. Methods that remain unchanged after full randomization are independent of the model.

---

**Data randomization test (Adebayo et al.)**: A sanity check that trains the model on data with randomly permuted labels (breaking the data-label relationship) and computes saliency on the same inputs. A faithful method should produce different saliency maps because the model has learned a random function, not the original task. Methods that produce similar maps before and after label permutation are independent of the data.

---

**Edge detector test**: Adebayo et al. compare saliency maps to the output of a Sobel edge detector applied to the input. Methods whose output correlates strongly with the Sobel filter are essentially detecting image edges, not model-specific features.

---

**Guided BackProp (GBP)**: A saliency variant that zeros negative gradients at each ReLU *during the backward pass* (in addition to blocking gradients through inactive forward neurons). Produces visually sharp maps but fails both Adebayo sanity checks — it is equivalent to denoised gradient on the input image, largely independent of the model.

---

**Guided Grad-CAM**: The product of Guided BackProp and Grad-CAM, point-wise. Inherits GBP's sharpness and Grad-CAM's coarse localization, but also inherits GBP's sanity check failure.

---

**Vanilla gradient (sanity check pass)**: Standard $\partial S_c / \partial I$ passes the model parameter randomization test (maps change under randomization) and the data randomization test. Not visually clean, but faithful.

---

**Grad-CAM (sanity check pass)**: Passes the model parameter randomization test at the inspected layer. Partially passes the data randomization test. Recommended over Guided Grad-CAM when faithfulness is required.

---

**Infidelity** (Yeh et al., 2019): A faithfulness metric that measures how well a saliency map predicts the output change caused by perturbations:

```math
\text{INFD}(\Phi, f, x) = \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{I}, \delta) \sim \mu}\left[\left(f(x) - f(x - \mathbf{I}\delta) - \Phi(x)^\top \mathbf{I}\delta\right)^2\right]
```

Lower is better; measures how faithfully $\Phi(x)$ predicts the model's response to perturbations $\mathbf{I}\delta$.

---

## Intuition

Attribution methods (gradient, IG, SHAP) answer "which features drove this decision?" Counterfactual explanations answer a different question: "what is the minimal change that would flip this decision?" For a loan applicant denied credit, a counterfactual is "if your income were $8,000 higher, you would have been approved." This is actionable in a way that "income was the top feature" is not — it specifies a direction and magnitude, not just a signal.

The sanity check results are arguably the most important empirical finding in the XAI literature. Adebayo et al. showed that Guided BackProp — one of the most visually appealing and widely-used gradient visualization methods — produces maps that are statistically indistinguishable from the Sobel edge detector output, and do not change when the model is completely randomized. This means the maps reveal information about the input image's edges, not about what the model has learned. An explanation method that is model-independent cannot, by definition, explain the model.

## How it works

### FIDO: Filling In the Other

FIDO frames counterfactual explanation as constrained optimization over a spatial mask. The key innovation over prior methods (LIME occlusion, Meaningful Perturbation) is the use of a *generative infiller*: instead of replacing masked pixels with grey or Gaussian noise, FIDO uses a Context-Aware GAN (CA-GAN) to generate semantically coherent replacements conditioned on the unmasked context. This keeps the perturbed input close to the data manifold, avoiding the off-manifold artifacts that plague simpler perturbation methods.

The mask $\mathbf{m}$ is the optimization variable. FIDO relaxes $\mathbf{m}$ from binary to continuous $[0,1]$, applies a total variation (TV) regularizer to encourage spatial coherence, and optimizes using Adam with the reparameterization trick for the Bernoulli mask distribution.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F
from torch import Tensor
from typing import Callable

def fido_sdr_mask(
    predict_fn: Callable[[Tensor], Tensor],   # model: [1,3,H,W] -> [1,num_classes]
    infill_fn: Callable[[Tensor, Tensor], Tensor],  # GAN: (img, mask) -> infilled img
    image: Tensor,                             # [1, 3, H, W]
    target_class: int,
    confidence_threshold: float = 0.5,
    mask_l1_weight: float = 1e-3,
    tv_weight: float = 1e-4,
    num_steps: int = 300,
    lr: float = 0.05,
) -> Tensor:                                   # binary mask [1, 1, H, W]
    """
    FIDO Smallest Deletion Region (SDR) optimization.
    Returns a spatial mask where 1 = deleted region.
    SDR objective: minimize mask size s.t. confidence < threshold after deletion.
    """
    _, _, H, W = image.shape
    # Initialize logit mask (sigmoid → continuous mask in [0,1])
    mask_logits = torch.zeros(1, 1, H, W, requires_grad=True, device=image.device)
    optimizer = torch.optim.Adam([mask_logits], lr=lr)

    for step in range(num_steps):
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        mask = torch.sigmoid(mask_logits)   # [1, 1, H, W] in (0,1)

        # Infilled image: deleted region replaced with GAN output
        with torch.no_grad():
            infill = infill_fn(image, mask)           # [1, 3, H, W]

        # Composite: mask=1 → infill, mask=0 → original
        composite = image * (1 - mask) + infill * mask   # [1, 3, H, W]

        logits = predict_fn(composite)               # [1, num_classes]
        conf = torch.softmax(logits, dim=1)[0, target_class]

        # SDR loss: confidence should drop below threshold
        conf_loss = F.relu(conf - confidence_threshold)   # 0 when satisfied

        # Sparsity: minimize the mask area
        l1_loss = mask.mean()

        # Total variation: encourage spatially contiguous mask
        tv_loss = (
            (mask[:, :, 1:, :] - mask[:, :, :-1, :]).abs().mean()
            + (mask[:, :, :, 1:] - mask[:, :, :, :-1]).abs().mean()
        )

        loss = conf_loss + mask_l1_weight * l1_loss + tv_weight * tv_loss
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()

    with torch.no_grad():
        binary_mask = (torch.sigmoid(mask_logits) > 0.5).float()
    return binary_mask
```

> [!NOTE]
> The CA-GAN infiller is the hardest part to implement in practice. FIDO's original paper trains a separate CA-GAN on each dataset (ImageNet, Places365). For most users, a drop-in replacement is `torch.nn.functional.interpolate` for blurring or a pretrained inpainting model (e.g., LaMa from Samsung Research). The specific infiller affects the SDR output — blurring-based infill tends to produce larger SDRs because blurred regions remain partially informative to the model.

### The Adebayo sanity checks

The two sanity checks are simple and require only a standard model implementation. They serve as go/no-go tests for saliency methods before deployment.

**Model parameter randomization test:**

1. Start with the fully trained model.
2. Randomly reinitialize the last layer's weights (all higher layers intact).
3. Compute saliency maps for several test images.
4. Repeat, now reinitializing the last two layers.
5. Continue cascading inward to layer 1.
6. Plot the SSIM (structural similarity) or Spearman rank correlation between the original saliency maps and the cascaded-randomized versions.

A faithful method should show monotonically *decreasing* similarity as more layers are randomized. Guided BackProp and Guided Grad-CAM show near-constant high similarity even after full randomization — their maps are essentially edge detectors.

**Data randomization test:**

1. Train a copy of the model on data with randomly shuffled labels (same inputs, random targets).
2. Compute saliency maps on the same test images with both the original and the label-shuffled model.
3. Compare the two sets of maps.

A faithful method should produce substantially different maps because the two models have learned fundamentally different functions (one generalized, one random). Methods that produce similar maps are revealing input structure, not model behaviour.

```python
import torch
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import spearmanr
from torchvision import models

def model_parameter_randomization_test(
    model: torch.nn.Module,
    saliency_fn: callable,   # (model, x, target_class) -> np.ndarray of shape [H, W]
    test_images: torch.Tensor,  # [N, 3, H, W]
    target_classes: list[int],
) -> list[float]:
    """
    Cascades random re-initialization through network layers.
    Returns list of Spearman correlation values (original vs. randomized saliency).
    """
    import copy

    # Compute original saliency maps
    original_sals = [
        saliency_fn(model, test_images[i:i+1], target_classes[i])
        for i in range(len(target_classes))
    ]

    # Get named parameters in reverse order (last layer first)
    param_names = list(dict(model.named_parameters()).keys())[::-1]

    correlations = []
    randomized_model = copy.deepcopy(model)

    for param_name in param_names:
        # Randomly reinitialize this parameter
        param = dict(randomized_model.named_parameters())[param_name]
        with torch.no_grad():
            param.data = torch.randn_like(param.data)

        rand_sals = [
            saliency_fn(randomized_model, test_images[i:i+1], target_classes[i])
            for i in range(len(target_classes))
        ]

        # Average Spearman rank correlation across test images
        corrs = []
        for orig, rand in zip(original_sals, rand_sals):
            rho, _ = spearmanr(orig.flatten(), rand.flatten())
            corrs.append(rho)
        correlations.append(float(np.mean(corrs)))

    return correlations   # should decrease monotonically for faithful methods


def data_randomization_test(
    model_class: type,
    model_kwargs: dict,
    train_fn: callable,     # (model, images, labels) -> trained model
    train_images: torch.Tensor,
    train_labels: torch.Tensor,
    saliency_fn: callable,
    test_images: torch.Tensor,
    target_classes: list[int],
) -> tuple[list[np.ndarray], list[np.ndarray]]:
    """
    Returns (original_saliency_maps, random_label_saliency_maps).
    Faithful methods should produce structurally different outputs.
    """
    # Train on original labels
    orig_model = train_fn(model_class(**model_kwargs), train_images, train_labels)
    orig_sals = [saliency_fn(orig_model, test_images[i:i+1], target_classes[i])
                 for i in range(len(target_classes))]

    # Train on shuffled labels
    shuffled_labels = train_labels[torch.randperm(len(train_labels))]
    rand_model = train_fn(model_class(**model_kwargs), train_images, shuffled_labels)
    rand_sals = [saliency_fn(rand_model, test_images[i:i+1], target_classes[i])
                 for i in range(len(target_classes))]

    return orig_sals, rand_sals
```

> [!CAUTION]
> Do not deploy Guided BackProp, Guided Grad-CAM, or any method that fails the model parameter randomization test in a regulated or safety-critical setting. These methods' maps are essentially edge detectors with better aesthetics — they are not faithful explanations of the model. Use vanilla gradient, Grad-CAM, or Integrated Gradients instead, and verify they pass the sanity checks on your specific architecture.

## Math

The FIDO SDR optimization problem can be written as a constrained integer program relaxed to continuous optimization:

```math
\min_{\mathbf{m} \in [0,1]^{H \times W}} \sum_{i,j} m_{ij} + \lambda_{\text{TV}} \cdot \text{TV}(\mathbf{m})
```

```math
\text{s.t.} \quad f_c\!\left(I \odot (1 - \mathbf{m}) + G(\mathbf{m}) \odot \mathbf{m}\right) < \tau
```

where $G(\mathbf{m}) = \text{CA-GAN}(I, \mathbf{m})$ is the generative infill conditioned on the mask, and $\text{TV}(\mathbf{m}) = \sum_{i,j}\sqrt{(m_{i+1,j} - m_{i,j})^2 + (m_{i,j+1} - m_{i,j})^2}$ is the total variation regularizer.

The infidelity metric from Yeh et al. for an attribution vector $\Phi(x) \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

```math
\text{INFD}(\Phi, f, x) = \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{I}, \delta) \sim \mu}\left[\left(f(x) - f(x - \mathbf{I}\delta) - \Phi(x)^\top (\mathbf{I}\delta)\right)^2\right]
```

Lower INFD = more faithful. Integrated Gradients achieves lower INFD than vanilla gradient on most architectures; SHAP achieves the lowest because it explicitly satisfies Completeness.

## Real-world example

The counterfactual explanation use case is most compelling in algorithmic decision-making. Here is a minimal demonstrating the generation of counterfactual explanations for a trained tabular classifier using the `dice-ml` library, which implements the DiCE (Diverse Counterfactual Explanations) method.

```python
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import dice_ml
from dice_ml import Dice
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

# Synthetic loan dataset
np.random.seed(0)
n = 2000
df = pd.DataFrame({
    "age":            np.random.randint(22, 65, n),
    "income":         np.clip(np.random.normal(50000, 20000, n), 15000, None),
    "credit_score":   np.clip(np.random.normal(650, 80, n), 300, 850).astype(int),
    "loan_amount":    np.random.randint(5000, 50000, n),
    "num_defaults":   np.random.poisson(0.3, n),
})
# Label: defaults ~ high loan/income ratio + low credit score
logit = (0.5 * df.num_defaults + 0.01 * (df.loan_amount / df.income)
         - 0.005 * (df.credit_score - 500))
df["approved"] = (1 / (1 + np.exp(-logit + 0.2)) > 0.45).astype(int)

X = df.drop("approved", axis=1)
y = df["approved"]

# Train classifier
clf = Pipeline([
    ("scaler", StandardScaler()),
    ("rf", RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=42)),
])
clf.fit(X, y)

# DiCE counterfactuals
d = dice_ml.Data(
    dataframe=df,
    continuous_features=["age", "income", "credit_score", "loan_amount"],
    outcome_name="approved",
)
m = dice_ml.Model(model=clf, backend="sklearn")
exp = Dice(d, m, method="random")

# Explain a denied applicant
denied_applicant = pd.DataFrame([{
    "age": 28, "income": 32000, "credit_score": 590,
    "loan_amount": 20000, "num_defaults": 1,
}])

dice_exp = exp.generate_counterfactuals(
    denied_applicant, total_CFs=3, desired_class="opposite"
)
dice_exp.visualize_as_dataframe(show_only_changes=True)
# Output: 3 minimal changes that would flip the decision to "approved"
# e.g., "increase credit_score to 680 OR decrease loan_amount to 12000"
```

> [!TIP]
> For production counterfactual generation, prefer methods that constrain explanations to be *actionable* — changes the applicant can actually make. DiCE supports feature constraints (e.g., `age` is non-actionable; `credit_score` can only increase). Without actionability constraints, counterfactuals often suggest impossible changes (e.g., "be 10 years older").

## In practice

**The sanity check failure of Guided BackProp is architecture-specific in a surprising way.** The failure occurs because Guided BackProp effectively computes the deconvolution of the input through the network's linear weights, ignoring the nonlinear activations. This is equivalent to the backward pass of a network where all ReLUs are open — which, in the limit of a deep linear network, is just a series of transposed matrix multiplications applied to the input image, producing an edge detector. For networks with very heavy nonlinearities (binary activation networks, very deep sigmoid networks), GBP behavior differs, but the failure generalizes across ReLU architectures.

**FIDO's generative infiller is the bottleneck for adoption.** The CA-GAN must be trained per dataset, adding significant engineering overhead. In practice, LaMa (Large Mask Inpainting) or Stable Diffusion inpainting are viable drop-in replacements that are pretrained on large datasets and require no per-dataset training. The quality of the infill directly affects the validity of the SDR/SSR — poor infills that produce obviously synthetic regions will cause the model to respond to the synthesis artifact, not to the absence of the original content.

**Counterfactuals alone do not provide unique attribution.** There are typically infinitely many counterfactuals for any decision — DiCE generates diverse sets precisely because of this. The choice of which counterfactual to present to an end user is itself a decision that can be gamed. An adversarial lender could present counterfactuals that suggest impossible changes, technically satisfying the GDPR right-to-explanation requirement while providing no actionable guidance.

> [!WARNING]
> The infidelity metric (Yeh et al.) and the sanity checks (Adebayo et al.) measure different aspects of faithfulness and can disagree. A method can have low infidelity (predicts perturbation responses well) but fail the randomization test (its maps don't change under model randomization). Both tests should be run before deploying any saliency method.

**Counterfactual explanations can leak information about the model's decision boundary.** Generating counterfactuals for a black-box model via repeated queries (as in LIME or DiCE's random method) effectively maps the decision boundary in the neighbourhood of the query point. In adversarial settings, an attacker could use counterfactual generation queries to reconstruct the model or extract training data. For deployed APIs, rate-limiting explanation queries is advisable.

## Pitfalls

- **Wrong belief: Visually appealing saliency maps are faithful saliency maps.** Correction: Adebayo et al. showed that the most visually appealing methods (Guided BackProp, Guided Grad-CAM) are the least faithful. Visual coherence is a product of the input image structure, not the model.
- **Wrong belief: The sanity checks are too strict — no useful method should be discarded.** Correction: A saliency method that is model-independent is an edge detector, not an explanation. If it passes visual inspection but fails the sanity checks, it cannot be used to diagnose model failures, debug spurious correlations, or satisfy regulatory explanation requirements.
- **Wrong belief: Counterfactual explanations are always causally valid.** Correction: Counterfactuals describe changes in the model's prediction, not changes in the real-world outcome. If the model has learned a spurious correlation, the counterfactual will reflect that correlation, not the true causal mechanism.
- **Wrong belief: SDR = SSR always partition the image.** Correction: SDR and SSR are not complements. The SSR is the smallest region *sufficient* for the decision; the SDR is the smallest region whose removal *necessary* changes it. For complex images with multiple diagnostic regions, $\text{SDR} \cap \text{SSR}$ may be empty (neither region explains the other).
- **Wrong belief: Running sanity checks once validates a method globally.** Correction: The randomization tests validate a method on a specific architecture and dataset. A method that passes on ResNet-50/ImageNet may fail on a different architecture or domain. Sanity checks should be rerun when the model or domain changes.

## Open questions

- **Unified faithfulness evaluation**: No single metric combines the sanity checks, infidelity, and deletion/insertion curves into a single overall faithfulness score. Active research area; see Hedström et al. (2023) Quantus library for a partial synthesis.
- **Counterfactuals under distribution shift**: Current counterfactual methods generate counterfactuals with respect to the current model, which may be wrong in the future (after model retraining). Temporally stable counterfactuals are unstudied.
- **Saliency for generative models**: Diffusion models and LLMs generate rather than classify. The concept of "which input feature caused this output token" requires a new formalism — attribution in a generation context is an open problem.
